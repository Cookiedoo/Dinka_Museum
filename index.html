Curator's Statement

Every single rescue in this story is financed by a life or a debt.

Aluel and Her Loving Father is usually read as a folktale about an orphan delivered from a cruel stepmother by divine intervention. The tale is found summarized in many areas, but the true narrative is much more depraved than those summaries let on. The Sun saves Aluel but demands a brown cow-calf from her bride-wealth on pain of curse; Chol regains his daughter and kills his own wife; Ring wins his bride and kills his own mother for her hide, then kills his own father; the Sun's own wives lose the child they raised.

Even the happy ending is taxed — Aluel bears many children, but she "constantly cries" from the day her mother-in-law was slaughtered for her. The five artifacts in this exhibit refuse to soften that pattern. They render visible what the tale refuses to hide: in this world, rescue isn't gentle, and deliverance often has a painful price.

The tale's own theology holds together what modern retellings pull apart. Fate acts in terrible ways. God wills in ways unknown. Debts bind and compound. Violence purchases what love cannot.
